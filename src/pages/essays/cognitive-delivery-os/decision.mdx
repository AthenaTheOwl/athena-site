A decision record is the answer to "why did we do it this way?" — written down at the moment the answer is fresh, kept around for the moment it stops being obvious. Each product repo keeps `decisions/DEC-*.md` files. The contract is `ops/schemas/decision.schema.json`. The validator is `scripts/validate_decisions.py` in each repo.

## What the record holds

A decision has six required parts:

- `decision_id` — a stable id like `DEC-CIT-002`.
- `spec_id` — the spec the decision serves.
- `requirement_id` — the requirement the decision resolves.
- `alternatives` — the options that lost, with one sentence each on why.
- `evidence` — pointers to the experiments or sources that grounded the choice.
- `reversibility` — what would have to happen to undo this, and how expensive that would be.

The reversibility field is the load-bearing one. A decision that doesn't name its reversibility cost is a decision that pretends the future has no opinion. Most decisions are reversible at a known cost; the act of naming the cost is the act of acknowledging the future has a vote.

## A concrete example

Procurement-lab's `decisions/CIT-002.md` is the citation-shape evolution path:

```markdown
---
decision_id: DEC-CIT-002
spec_id: specs/0001-lab-engine/
requirement_id: R-LAB-0001-7
status: accepted
date: 2026-04-12
reversible: true
reversibility_cost: medium
---

# Citation shape evolution: from offsets to ranges

## Decision

Citations are stored as `{document_id, start, end}` byte ranges instead
of `{document_id, offset, length}` pairs.

## Alternatives that lost

- **Offset + length.** Cheaper to store; harder to compose when two
  citations cover adjacent text.
- **Document id only.** Cheapest; useless for citation faithfulness eval.

## Evidence

- The four-suite eval in supplier-risk-rag-agent showed range-shaped
  citations compose into multi-source claims; offset+length forced the
  evaluator to recompute the end on every overlap check.

## Reversibility

Reversible. A migration script can convert range to offset+length in
O(n) over the citation table; the four-suite eval would need its
overlap-check rewritten.
```

Supplier-risk-rag-agent's most recent commit amended that exact decision's reversibility note — a `DEC-CIT-002-amendment-reversibility-mitigation` sibling file that points at the original via `amends:` and captures a forward-looking mitigation without overwriting the original record. That's what an amendment looks like in operation: the prior decision stays; the new one is a sibling artifact.

## Why decisions are records, not commit messages

A commit message answers "what changed." A decision record answers "why did we choose this, and what else did we consider?" The commit message lives inside the diff; the decision lives outside it, points at it, and survives a force-push. A team that puts the rationale in the commit message loses the rationale the next time someone rewrites history.

## What the audit asks

The portfolio audit asks four questions about decisions every week:

- Which requirements have a spec but no decision yet?
- Which decisions point at a spec or requirement that no longer exists?
- Which decisions are open (`status: proposed`) more than 14 days?
- Which decisions are reversible but haven't been re-validated since they landed?

The answers go into `ops/portfolio-health.md` every Monday. The repo whose decision drifted has a week to either close the gap or file a follow-up decision that explains the delay.
