One Vickrey-Clarke-Groves mechanism isn't a procurement strategy. It's a tool. Mechanism design is the discipline of picking tools — and picking when each one breaks.

[The coordination gap](/essays/the-coordination-gap/) argued that mechanism design recovers joint value without forcing either side to disclose its costs. True in theory. In practice, the textbook version of VCG fails before you ship it: full transfers can blow your budget; suppliers learn to inflate stated costs to game inflated transfers; running the full counterfactual per supplier per round costs real compute; and a planner who can override the optimization sovereignty nullifies the dominant-strategy guarantee. The math is right; the operating loop needs work.

The operational refinements are themselves design choices. Four worth naming:

**α clipping.** Instead of paying the full VCG transfer `T_i = max(E_i, 0)`, pay `α · T_i` with α between 0 and 1. The math weakens — dominant-strategy incentive compatibility becomes approximate — but the practical anti-gaming pressure survives, and the spend stays bounded. The choice of α encodes how much you trust the vendor base and how much budget you're willing to spend to enforce honesty. α isn't a tuning parameter. It's a posture.

**Reliability multipliers.** Instead of accepting vendor-stated capacity at face value, the planner system applies a historical-adherence weight. The vendor doesn't have to disclose its cost structure; the planner uses the vendor's past behavior as a prior on stated capacity. This sidesteps the asymmetric-information problem from a different angle — not "make truth-telling worth it" but "the system already knows enough of the truth to discount obvious overpromising." The choice of how much weight history gets is, again, a design decision.

**ε-frontier.** Instead of returning one optimal plan, return the top-K within ε of optimal. Lets the planner choose between optimal-but-thin and slightly-suboptimal-but-robust. Transfers are recomputed consistently for whichever the planner picks. The ε isn't a UX nicety — it's the choice to preserve human judgment over pure optimization. Some categories want ε = 0 (trust the math). Some want ε large (preserve options). The right setting changes as the system matures.

**Decoy demand.** During pilots, inject scenarios with known answers — known-cheap routing, known-fragile suppliers, known-collusion-prone categories — and watch whether the vendor responses match the expected pattern. Catches systematic misreporting that the equilibrium math assumes away. The decoys aren't a feature; they're an audit trail.

Each of these refinements is a *rule choice*. None is just an algorithm tweak. α encodes posture toward gaming. Reliability priors encode trust in behavioral history. ε encodes preference for optionality. Decoys encode tolerance for hidden adversarial behavior. The math underneath is the same VCG framework — but the operationalization is plural.

Plural is the key word. There isn't one mechanism for procurement. There's a family of mechanisms parameterized by the four levers above, and the right family member for *which category* and *which season* changes as the system runs. Holiday seasonal volatility wants different α than steady-state purchasing. New-vendor onboarding wants more decoys; mature trusted vendors need fewer. Single-source categories get reliability priors that anchor heavily on incumbent history; categories with three credible alternates can afford more aggressive ε.

You don't pick a mechanism. You pick a family with parameters that adapt.

And then you watch the system run. After a quarter, the data tells you which α was right (did transfers stay in budget? did vendor inflation drop?), which reliability priors were too generous (which vendors missed commitments at higher rates than the prior expected?), which decoys caught real collusion vs noise (were the misreports concentrated in specific categories?). Each answer feeds back into next quarter's parameter set. The mechanism *is* the practice of running it.

This is the part that doesn't make it into the textbook chapter. The textbook stops at "this mechanism has dominant-strategy incentive compatibility." The job stops at "this parameter choice fits this category this quarter, and here's how we'll know to update it." VCG is the foundation. Mechanism design is the practice. They aren't the same thing.

The [procurement-negotiation-lab](https://procurement-negotiation-lab.vercel.app/) teaches the foundation — one path through one set of mechanisms with default parameters. It's a fixed sample of what the math looks like in operation. The harder question — the one a planning org actually has to answer — is how the practice gets built around the math. What's the cadence for retuning α? Who owns the reliability-prior update? When does a decoy-detected misreport escalate to contract renegotiation versus algorithmic correction?

Those aren't math questions. They're product and operating questions. They sit downstream of the mechanism choice and upstream of the actual cost savings. A PMT shipping procurement systems lives in that downstream-of-math, upstream-of-savings layer. The math is the ticket to the discussion. The discipline is everything after.

Mechanism design teaches you that the rules of the transaction matter as much as the substance of the transaction. Operational mechanism design teaches you that the rules themselves are decisions to be made — and remade — by the people running the system. The lab is the foundation. The next layer is the work.
